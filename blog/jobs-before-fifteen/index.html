Gen X Work Ethic April 11, 2026

Jobs Before Fifteen
(And the Offshore Year)

Before I was 15, I'd already had a full career. No permission slip. No guidance counselor. No one explaining the steps. Just a kid who needed things done and figured out how to do them.

RG
Rob Gaudet
Founder · Cajun Navy · TextOS

People ask where the work ethic comes from. They want a neat answer — a mentor, a moment, a turning point. There wasn't one. There was just a long series of situations where nobody else was going to handle it, so I did.

Here's the actual timeline.

The Early Jobs

  • Age 3 Used to pick up neighbors' newspapers from their driveways and knock on their doors and try to sell them back. First sales job. No shame, no fear.
  • Age 8 Parents divorced. Grass at mom's house was a foot tall. She asked me to mow it. I walked a mile to my grandmother's house alone, borrowed her lawn mower, and pushed it all the way back. The grass was so tall the mower kept dying. I figured out on my own to only mow half the deck width so I could keep moving. Nobody taught me that. I worked it out.
  • Age 10 Signed myself up to play soccer at the YMCA. Paid for it myself. Bought my own cleats. Rode my bike to weekday practices and Saturday morning games. That was 46 years ago. I still play soccer every week.
  • Age 13 Paper route. Bike with a loaded paper bag over my shoulders — papers front and back. Had to memorize every house on the route. Took two weeks. Until I had it memorized, I stopped in front of every house and checked the address list. Once a month, went door-to-door collecting money. Kept a percentage.
  • Soon after Parents' friend asked if I could work at Orange Julius in Acadiana Mall. Did that for a few months.
  • Age 15 School ski trip. Five kids in the family — parents couldn't afford it. I had to pay for it myself. A friend got me a job at Wendy's. Worked long enough to pay for the trip, then quit. She then got me a job at a frame shop where I worked every day after school and weekends for over a year. Then: Chess King as a salesperson. All before graduation.

The grass was so tall the mower kept dying. I figured out on my own to only mow half the deck width so I could keep moving. Nobody taught me that. I worked it out.

— Rob Gaudet, age 8

The Pattern No One Named

Looking back, the through-line is obvious. Every single time, the situation required something and I found a way to supply it. Nobody handed me a job description. Nobody explained the system. I just moved toward whatever needed doing.

At 3, I understood that people valued their newspaper and I had one. At 8, I understood that a lawn mower problem was an engineering problem — if it keeps dying, change your approach. At 13, I understood that a paper route was a memory problem before it was a logistics problem.

This is what they now call a growth mindset. We called it Saturday.

Five kids in the family. Parents couldn't afford the ski trip. So I paid for it myself. That's not exceptional — that's just what you did.

— Rob Gaudet

The Offshore Year

After high school, a girlfriend's dad got me a job offshore. I flew on helicopters to oil rigs. Worked in the kitchen and maintained the bunkhouse. Seven different rigs over a year — two weeks out, one week home.

Made real money for the first time. Real, physical, consequence-having money. Not tip jar money or envelope-stuffing money. A paycheck that reflected 80-hour weeks in a place where the floor moved under you.

I showed my dad my paycheck. He decided I needed to pay rent.

I was heartbroken for about 15 seconds.

Then I got my own apartment. I have never lived at home again.

I was heartbroken for 15 seconds — then got my own apartment. I've never lived at home again.

— Rob Gaudet

What It Actually Taught Me

Every generation has its version of this story. The specifics vary — paper routes became gig apps, Orange Julius became DoorDash, offshore became remote contract work. But the core transaction is the same: you have a need, the world has a mechanism, and you figure out how to work the mechanism.

What the jobs actually taught me wasn't how to stock a frame shop or how to work a kitchen on a rig. They taught me that problems are solvable — not because someone will solve them for you, but because the tools are usually right there if you look at the problem directly enough.

The mower kept dying. So I changed my approach. That logic has applied to every significant thing I've done since — from building the original Tea Party Patriots website in four days to founding the Cajun Navy during a disaster with no playbook and no budget.

You figure it out. You keep moving. The deck width changes. You adapt.

That's it. That's the whole thing.
