The Tea Party
Origin Story

March 26, 2009. 9:05 PM CST. A cold email to two men I didn't know. A reply that came back within hours asking if I could start tonight. Two words: "Sure thing." That's how it started — pulled directly from my Gmail archive.

RG
Rob Gaudet
Founder · Cajun Navy · TextOS

I've been going back through my Gmail archives — the folders labeled "TPP - Pre TPP" and "TPP - Email History." I want to document this accurately, because the way movements start is usually more interesting and more instructive than the way they're remembered.

Here's what actually happened.

The First Email

Date: March 26, 2009 — 9:05 PM CST Subject: Database guy from the red river. From: Rob Gaudet, Bossier City, LA To: Michael Patrick Leahy · Eric Odom

I'm a software consultant from Bossier City, LA. I've been watching the grassroots Tea Party movement gain momentum and I'd like to help with data infrastructure.

I've been monitoring Obama's barackobama.com as a registered member — studying their grassroots tools. I think I can build something similar.

Sign-off: "P. J. O'Rourke — 'If government were a product, selling it would be illegal.'"

That was the pitch. A cold email to two people I didn't know, sent on a Thursday night from Bossier City, Louisiana. I'd been watching the Tea Party build steam and I thought: I have the skills this needs. I should offer them.

The Reply That Changed Everything

Date: March 26, 2009 — same night, within hours From: Michael Patrick Leahy

"Can you lead a project starting now to create a comprehensive real-time updated spreadsheet with ALL April 15 Tea Party info?... Will you accept this task? If so, can you start tonight?"

His reply came back within hours. "Can you start tonight?" My answer: "Sure thing." Two words. That's how it started.

— Rob Gaudet

Two words. That's how it started.

Four Days to a Working Platform

I was already building by March 27. Emails flying with Michael Leahy about database double-counts, volunteer coordination, Google Forms for organizer data collection. By March 29, I was on a tech conference call and sending detailed notes to Mark Meckler — who would become one of the movement's most prominent co-founders.

Then, on March 30, I sent the pitch for the actual website:

Date: March 30, 2009 From: Rob Gaudet

"I have made significant progress. Tea Parties can be added and approved. They can be listed by state. Also you can enter a zip code and it will show events within a 500 mile radius."

In four days — working from my home office in Bossier City — I had built zip-code radius search, state filtering, event approval workflows, and the skeleton of what would become TeaPartyPatriots.org.

No team. No budget. No office. Just a problem worth solving and a deadline that mattered.

The Inner Circle

By mid-2009, I was embedded with the full TPP leadership:

  • Jenny Beth Martin Co-founder. Made Rob an admin on the Google Group at midnight: "i'll give it to you now."
  • Mark Meckler Co-founder. Rob's primary tech liaison from day one.
  • Amy Kremer Later Chairman of Tea Party Express.
  • Diana Reimer National coordinator.
  • Debbie Dooley Prominent state organizer.

By July 2009, I was running the TPP Technology Team — fielding dozens of volunteer web developers wanting to contribute. Screening them, assigning tasks, managing a remote team for a movement that had no budget and no roadmap.

2010: Full Force

By 2010, the emails show me in national board calls, litigation strategy discussions, political conversations around Senate primaries in Nevada, Kentucky, Colorado, Florida, and Delaware.

I wasn't just the webmaster. I was an operational core member. The difference between those two things is the difference between someone who maintains the infrastructure and someone who helps decide what the infrastructure should serve.

This was full-time work, nights and weekends, from day one. Building infrastructure for a movement that had no guarantee of success. And I did it anyway.

— Rob Gaudet

What I Want People to Know About How Things Start

There's a version of this story that's been told a hundred ways: the Tea Party rose, it changed American politics, here are the names attached to it. That version is true but incomplete.

What the emails show — what I want on the record — is the texture of how something real gets built. A Thursday night cold email. A reply that came back the same night asking if you could start now. The decision to say yes before you had any idea what yes actually meant.

Most important things start that way. Not with a plan. Not with funding. Not with a team. With someone willing to say "sure thing" and stay up that night to figure out what they just agreed to.

The movement was already there. I just gave it a spine. That's all infrastructure ever does — and it's almost never recognized as the thing that made the difference.

The emails know.
