History Organizing April 11, 2026

Tea Party Patriots —
National Coordinator

I built TeaPartyPatriots.org from my home office in Bossier City, Louisiana. 127,000 members. 6,000 events in 18 months. A million-email list. Yahoo News called it one of the defining political organizing stories of the Internet age. Here's what that actually looked like from the inside.

RG
Rob Gaudet
Founder · Cajun Navy · TextOS

I was a software consultant. I had clients. I had a mortgage. I had a family. And I watched a movement grow — something real, something spontaneous, something that clearly needed the kind of infrastructure I knew how to build.

So I built it.

That decision cost me $100,000 in forfeited income. It also put me at the center of what Yahoo News would later describe as the moment the right caught up with — and arguably surpassed — the left's digital organizing advantage.

The Numbers

6,000+ Events posted in 18 months
2,400 Local Tea Party groups, state by state
127K Registered members, hundreds joining daily
~1M Email list across related sites
436K Facebook fans — vs. 122K for the Democratic Party
$100K Personal income forfeited to do it full-time

Those aren't PR numbers. Those are what happened when I stopped taking client work entirely and went full-time for the movement. I became the paid national coordinator of Tea Party Patriots.

What the Internet Made Possible

The Tea Party was a genuine grassroots phenomenon — real frustration, real people, real energy that existed independent of any infrastructure. But infrastructure is what turns energy into movement. And in 2009, most of that infrastructure didn't exist yet for the right.

The left had learned how to use the internet for organizing. Howard Dean's campaign, then Obama's, had demonstrated what was possible. The right was behind. When I built TeaPartyPatriots.org — a place where supporters could post events, list rallies, find each other by zip code and state — the response was immediate and overwhelming.

"Even without the Internet, the Tea Party would still have grown, but it has been absolutely critical in allowing people to connect, share and promote their events."

— Rob Gaudet, as quoted in Yahoo News, ~2010

The technology itself wasn't exotic. Event listings. State directories. Email lists. The innovation was applying it to this particular moment, this particular need, at the right speed.

The Personal Cost

Here's what doesn't make it into the headline numbers: the cost.

Forfeiting $100,000 in personal income isn't a rounding error. That's a real number with real consequences — for my family, for my financial position, for everything downstream. I knew what I was giving up. I made the choice anyway.

"I've been pushing to enable citizens to hold government accountable. There's been a lot of pain and sacrifice with my family to make this happen. Our Founding Fathers talked about sacrificing fortune for our sacred honor. I have a better understanding now of what they were talking about."

— Rob Gaudet, Yahoo News

I'm not interested in martyrdom stories. But I think it's important to be honest about what civic action actually costs — because the sanitized version, where someone does something important and it's just inspiring and fine, doesn't prepare anyone for the reality of doing something important.

It costs. Sometimes it costs a lot. You do it anyway or you don't.

What It Taught Me About Organizing

The Tea Party experience shaped everything I've done since. The Cajun Navy. TextOS. Stability.org. Every organizing project I've been part of carries DNA from this one.

What I learned: infrastructure is not the movement, but the movement cannot scale without infrastructure. A thousand people showing up angry is a protest. Ten thousand people showing up with a directory, a contact list, event coordination tools, and a way to stay connected after the rally — that's a movement.

The internet didn't create the Tea Party. The internet gave the Tea Party the connective tissue it needed to become something that could actually change things.

That lesson applies to everything. Disasters. Civic engagement. Product companies. Any time you need people to coordinate at scale, the infrastructure question is the core question.

I've been answering versions of that question ever since.
