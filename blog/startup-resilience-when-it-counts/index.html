Startup Resilience:
What Innovate South Taught Me

A direct note to the founders, mentors, and investors who know my work — on what happened at Innovate South 2026, what Stability.org is really about, and where we go from here.

RG
Rob Gaudet
Founder, Stability.org · Builder, TextOS.ai & Reyla.ai · 30 years in technology

I want to share something with you directly — because you're someone whose work I respect and whose opinion matters to me. I'd rather you hear it with full context than piece it together from fragments.

Recently I pitched at Innovate South. It was the first time I've pitched this project publicly after 10 years of building it.

The deck was strong. Genuinely one of the sharpest pieces of communication I've put together on the work. I walked in prepared and confident. And then there was a technical issue — the clicker stopped advancing the slides early in the pitch and never synced back up.

I made the call to keep going. The visual story and my words were disconnected for most of the presentation. I improvised. I didn't win.

I want to be straightforward about that. But I also don't want the glitch to become the whole story — because it isn't.

What This Project Actually Is

Ten years. That's not hyperbole. The work that became what I pitched at Innovate South is Stability.org — a civic resilience technology platform built to help communities prepare for, respond to, and recover from disasters.

What I'm building now through Stability.org is the technology infrastructure behind that mission. The platform that doesn't just respond to disasters but helps communities build the resilience to withstand them. This isn't a startup I spun up last quarter. It's the culmination of a decade of learning what breaks, what holds, and what the gap is between the two.

Disaster response is a hard space to pitch. The ROI isn't clean. The urgency is episodic — audiences don't feel the weight of it until they're living through one. I knew that going in. It doesn't change what I'm building or why.

There were people in that room who have followed this work for years. Who showed up specifically to hear the pitch. They deserved a better version of it than what they got — and I intend to give it to them.

— Rob Gaudet

What I'm Taking From It

The most honest reflection I can offer: the work is real, the deck works, and the moment didn't go as planned. That combination is actually a perfect summary of the past ten years.

I said something to a colleague after walking off stage that I keep coming back to: this is exactly what startups are. Nothing goes as expected. Not the product, not the funding, not the pitch. The entire job is to keep moving when things go sideways — to find your footing and come back better. That's not just a lesson for founders. That's the mission. Resilience is what I'm building technology around.

The group that won were polished, prepared, and deserving. They also had the advantage of stage time with the actual equipment before the competition. That's how it should work for every team, and it's feedback I'll be sharing with the organizers.

What I'm doing next
  • The deck is ready and I'm glad to share it — just reach out.
  • I'm actively scheduling follow-up conversations with the investors and connections who were in the room.
  • The next pitch will be sharper, tested on real equipment, and delivered by someone who now has the experience of winging it in front of 250 people and surviving.
  • If you have thoughts on how to sharpen the pitch for the next room, I am genuinely all ears.

A Note on Persistence

I've mentored enough founders to know that the ones who get funded eventually aren't always the ones who had the cleanest pitch day. They're the ones who showed up again. And again. Who didn't let a bad afternoon become the end of the story.

I've had a lot of those afternoons over ten years of building this. I'm still here. More convinced than ever that this work matters.

More coming. This was round one.
