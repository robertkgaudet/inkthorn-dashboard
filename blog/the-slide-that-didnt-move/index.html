The Slide That Didn't Move
(And What It Taught Me)

I stood on stage at Innovate South in front of 250 investors and founders and pitched publicly for the first time in 10 years. The clicker stopped working. The slides never caught up. Here's what I learned — and what every founder needs to hear before they step on a stage.

RG
Rob Gaudet
Founder, Stability.org · Builder, TextOS.ai & Reyla.ai · 30 years in technology

The deck was ready. I was ready.

The clicker wasn't.

Thirty seconds into my pitch at Innovate South, I realized the slides on the screen behind me had stopped advancing. The clock was already running. Two hundred and fifty people were watching — including investors I'd been building relationships with for years, people who had been looking forward to finally hearing me talk about what I've spent the last decade building.

I had a split-second choice: stop the room and fumble through a tech fix in front of everyone, or keep going and figure it out.

I kept going.

The slides never caught up. My words never matched what the audience saw behind me. I had to ditch everything I'd rehearsed — the pacing, the visual cues, the structure I'd spent weeks sharpening — and just talk. Pure improvisation. In front of the biggest room I'd been in for this project. For the first time in ten years of building it.

I didn't win.

What Happened After the Stage

When it was over, I ran into a friend who runs university-level innovation programs in Louisiana. I told him what happened and then heard myself say it out loud for the first time: "This is exactly what startups are."

Nothing goes as planned. Not the product. Not the funding. Not the pitch. The question is never whether something goes wrong — it always does. The question is whether you keep moving when it does.

My wife said it best when I got home. She told me: use it to teach.

That's the thing about hard moments. They're not wasted if you turn them into something useful for someone else. So that's what I'm doing here.

Three Lessons Every Founder Needs Before They Step on a Stage

  • Test the tech. Then test it again. Not your laptop at home — the actual screen, the actual clicker, the actual stage setup. Ask the organizers explicitly. Demand it if you have to. Your three minutes are too hard-earned to lose to an HDMI handshake. The group that won my competition got stage time beforehand to practice with the real equipment. Every team deserves that. If you're organizing a pitch event, this is not optional — it's the baseline.
  • Know your material well enough to ditch the slides entirely. If your pitch only works with the visual crutch, it's not pitch-ready yet. The slides support the story. You are the story. If someone cut the power mid-sentence, could you still land the room? If not, keep rehearsing.
  • Resilience is part of the pitch. Investors aren't just evaluating your product. They're evaluating you. How you handle adversity on a stage tells them exactly how you'll handle adversity inside their portfolio company. Stay on your feet. It matters more than the slide deck.

The founders who get knocked sideways and find their footing anyway — those are the ones who deserve backing. That's what resilience actually looks like from the outside.

— Rob Gaudet

Disaster response is a hard space to pitch. The ROI isn't clean. The urgency is episodic — nobody feels the weight of it until they're living through one. I knew that going in. The audience was never going to be easy.

But there were people in that room who knew what I was building. Who had been following this project, this mission, the work that started a decade ago when I built Stability.org. They showed up specifically to hear me talk about it — and they didn't get the version they deserved.

That part stings. I'll be honest about that.

But the real story for me isn't the clicker. It's ten years of work that I believe in, and one rough afternoon on a stage, and the decision to come back better.

A note for pitch event organizers: When founders step onto your stage — especially founders whose work spans a decade — they deserve equal access to the equipment, the screens, and the rehearsal time. That's not a luxury. That's the baseline. One technical failure in a high-stakes moment can misrepresent years of real work to the exact investors that work needs to reach.

Common Questions About Startup Pitching

What should a founder do when presentation tech fails mid-pitch?
Keep going. Stopping to troubleshoot in front of investors loses momentum and signals fragility. Know your material well enough to pitch without slides. The content is yours — own it regardless of what the screen shows. If you can't tell your story without the deck, you don't know it well enough yet.
How should you prepare for a pitch competition stage?
Test the actual equipment — not your laptop at home. Request time on the real stage with the real clicker, real screen, and real projector setup before the event. Many pitch competition organizers will accommodate this. Demand it if necessary. Your preparation is too valuable to lose to a technical failure on the day.
What do investors look for beyond the pitch deck?
Investors evaluate founders as much as products. How you handle adversity on stage tells them exactly how you'll handle adversity inside their portfolio company. Composure under pressure, real-time adaptability, and resilience matter more than a polished deck.
