Rob Gaudet
New Orleans, Louisiana

"I didn't build TextOS for the press release. I built it because I've watched good people fail for lack of the right information at the right time — and I finally had the tools to do something about it."

Section 1 · 1987

Where it started — Ryan's Steakhouse

I was 18 years old and waiting tables at Ryan's Steakhouse. By 24 I was the general manager with 60 employees, most of them older than me.

What that job taught me — before the internet, before software, before any of this — was how a business actually runs. Every day I could walk to the cash register, pull a report, and know exactly where we stood. Sales by hour. Covers. Waste. That number told me what decisions to make before lunch.

I didn't know it then, but that was the most important thing I ever learned about running a business: real-time information makes good operators. The absence of it makes desperate ones.

Section 2 · 1995

The career change nobody saw coming

In 1995 my son was born. I was still working at Ryan's full-time. And I started teaching myself to code at night.

I don't know exactly what I was chasing — maybe I just knew the restaurant industry wasn't going to be enough, and software felt like leverage I didn't have yet. By 1997 I had fully changed careers. My first programming job was building ValueBin.com for East Texas Distributing.

I never looked back.

Section 3 · 1997–2026

Thirty years of building systems

For the next thirty years I built systems for businesses — large ones, enterprise-scale ones — to replace how they operated. Not to add a feature. To replace how they worked, end to end.

Before I ever wrote a line of architecture I would spend months interviewing the people doing the actual work. Hundreds of them. Because the fastest way to build the wrong system is to assume you already understand the problem.

That methodology — listen first, simplify ruthlessly, build for the person doing the job, not the person approving the budget — made or saved the companies I worked with hundreds of millions of dollars. I stopped counting.

Section 4 · The pain that never went away

What I kept seeing

Here's what I kept seeing, in every company, in every industry, at every scale: the people with the best ideas had the worst infrastructure. Not because they were bad operators. Because nobody had ever built them a real system.

A solopreneur running a side hustle doesn't have a team of analysts. They have a phone and a headache and a list of things they haven't gotten to yet. And the tools that exist were built by people who've never done payroll, never hired someone twice their age, never had to decide between making rent and buying inventory.

I have done all of those things.

I know what it actually feels like to run out of information when you need it most.

Section 5 · Why it exists

Why TextOS exists

I built TextOS sitting under an oak tree at lunch with my wife. No office. No venture money. No team of engineers. Just Telegram and thirty years of knowing exactly what a small-business owner needs and doesn't have.

TextOS is not magic. It is what I would have wanted in 1987, and in 1995, and in every company I ever walked into with a yellow legal pad and a lot of questions.

It is a system. Built for operators, by one.

Section 6 · The mission

What I am trying to do

AI is not going to save your business by itself. But it will give you the information and leverage you need — if someone builds it right, for the right person, for the right reasons.

That is what I am trying to do. I have made it my mission to help you get your business ready for the age of AI. Not with hype. With a system that actually works.

— Rob
