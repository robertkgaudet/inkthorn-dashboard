Generation I
I
c. 1575
Father of a people
♛
Jean (Jehan) Gaudet
b. 1575 · Martaizé, Vienne, France
✦ Abraham of All Acadian & Cajun People ✦
From one man — over 234,000 souls
France in the early 1600s was a kingdom of peasant farmers, religious wars, and colonial ambition. Jean left a small village in the Loire Valley and crossed the Atlantic to build something new — arriving in Acadia when it was little more than a few dozen families clinging to the edge of a continent. He outlived a century, watching his children and grandchildren root themselves into marshland soil that would define a people.
The patriarch of the entire Gaudet lineage. Arrived in Acadia aboard the ship St.-Jehan in 1636 with three children. Lived past age 100 and is listed in the 1671 census as "the oldest inhabitant of Port Royal." He has over 234,000 known descendants — the most of any founding Acadian father.

Denis Gaudet
b. c. 1625 · France
Son of Jean · Born in France
Denis crossed the Atlantic as a child of eleven, trading the Loire Valley for the wild marshes of Nova Scotia. He grew up in one of the world's youngest colonies — Port Royal — where the Acadian identity was just beginning to take shape. Farming the tidal marshlands with the unique dyking system the Acadians perfected, Denis lived a long and prosperous settler's life.
Born in France before the family emigrated to Acadia. Denis was one of three children Jean brought to the New World in 1636. He grew up in the colony at Port Royal and married Martine Gauthier around 1645. He lived into his mid-eighties and died at Port Royal in October 1709.

Pierre Gaudet
b. c. 1651 · Port Royal, Annapolis, Acadie
First Generation Born in Acadia
Pierre was born into a community that had begun to thrive. The Acadians of Port Royal were developing their own distinct culture — French in language and faith, but uniquely shaped by the New World. They built dykes, raised cattle, traded furs, and largely kept the peace between French and British colonial powers through careful neutrality. Pierre's generation was the first that had never known France at all.
Born in the colony — the first of this line truly Acadian by birth. Grew up in Port Royal during a time of competing British and French colonial claims. Married Anne Blanchard and established his family on the fertile marshlands of Port Royal. His family appears in both the 1686 and 1693 Acadian censuses.

Generation V
V
c. 1727
Uprooted, unbroken, moving forward
Louis Gaudet
b. c. 1727 · Beaubassin, Cumberland, Nova Scotia
⚑ Deported to France · 1759
In 1755, British colonial authorities enacted one of the most sweeping forced removals in North American history — placing Acadian families on ships and scattering them across the Atlantic world. Farms were burned, families were split, and an entire way of life was torn apart. Louis was deported to France, a land his family had left four generations before. But the Acadians were not broken. They found each other again.
Louis lived through the catastrophic Grand Dérangement. In 1755, the British forcibly expelled the Acadian people from their homeland. Louis was deported to France in 1759 — a land his ancestors had left over 120 years before. After years of exile, he eventually made the journey to Louisiana, arriving around 1789, joining thousands of fellow Acadians who had found their way to the bayous.

François Louis would carry the Gaudet name to the New World a second time — this time to stay.

The first of the line born in Louisiana — a true Cajun by birth. Plattenville on Bayou Lafourche was a tight-knit Acadian community. The family had finally put down roots after generations of displacement. He grew up speaking French, working the bayou, and building a new version of the Acadian life his ancestors had lost.
